# The Pact and the Parton
The last thing Fintan remembered before darkness took him was the smell of wet iron. Blood. Not all of it was his. The ambush had come quietly—too quietly even for him. Crossbows from the ridge, blades from the brush. Professionals. The kind that knew how to wait. He had protected the merchant as long as he could. He remembered breaking a wrist with his cane. He remembered the crunch of ribs beneath his elbow. He remembered the sound of someone choking on their own breath. Then something struck the side of his skull. And the forest vanished.

When the world returned, it did not return correctly. Fintan was lying on his back, yet he could feel wind across his face as if he were standing. The ground beneath him felt like wood but also like water. He heard a faint sound. Cards. The soft whisper of playing cards being shuffled. He sat up slowly. And for the first time since childhood, Fintan could see. Not clearly. Not like others did. It was more like sensing shapes made of moonlight. Across from him sat a woman at a small wooden table. She wore a deep green cloak. A hood shadowed her face. Between them sat a deck of cards.

“Ah,” she said cheerfully. “There you are.”

Her voice sounded amused, like someone watching a particularly entertaining play. Fintan said nothing. Old instincts from the Guard of the Banshee told him something very important: this is not a mortal.

“You fought very hard,” the woman said. She dealt two cards onto the table. “You should be dead.”

Fintan tilted his head slightly. “I often should be.”

The woman laughed softly. “Oh good. You’re interesting.” She slid the cards toward him. “You saved someone tonight.”

“I did my job.”

“Yes,” she said. “And that is exactly why I am here.”

Fintan reached forward slowly. His fingers touched the cards. They were warm. Alive somehow.

“Who are you?” he asked.

The woman lifted her hood. There was no face beneath it. Just shifting light—like sunlight through leaves. A thousand different expressions flickered there: laughter, boredom, curiosity, mischief.

“Oh,” she said casually. “I have many names.” She tapped the deck. “Tonight you may call me Lady Luck.”

Fintan remained still.

“Luck,” he said quietly. “I do not believe in luck.”

“Oh, you should,” she replied. “You’ve been surviving on it for centuries.” She began laying cards onto the table one by one. Each card showed a memory. A boy tripping on a root. Blood on bark. Blindness. Another card. A young warrior carving the tattoo of the Guard into his skin. Another. Chains. The smell of rot. Men laughing. Another. Fintan drunk in the forest, cursing the gods. Another. Him standing again. Learning to track. Learning to listen. Another. Tonight’s battle. The woman leaned back. “You are fascinating,” she said. “You lose everything. Again and again. And every time you stand back up.”

Fintan remained silent.

“You see,” she continued, “most gamblers eventually stop playing.”

“They break.”

“You?” She leaned forward. “You keep drawing cards.”

She gathered the cards again. “Let’s play a game.”

Fintan sighed quietly. “Of course.”

She dealt two cards again. “Your life,” she said, tapping one card. “Your future,” tapping the other. “And here is my offer.” She pushed the deck toward him. “I will give you power. Magic. Luck. The ability to twist fate just a little.”

Fintan tilted his head. “What do you gain?”

She grinned. “Oh, that’s the best part. You continue exactly what you already do. Walk dangerous roads. Protect travelers. Take ridiculous risks. Get yourself into trouble. You live a life where anything can happen. Because boring lives are the only thing I truly dislike.”

“Every game has rules,” Fintan said.

“Yes,” she said. “And yours are simple.” She placed the deck into his hands. “You must never refuse the gamble. Help those who risk the road. Trust chance when logic fails. And from time to time… cause a little chaos. A trick here. A stolen moment there. A well-timed prank against someone too proud. Also, if fate places you at a table… you must play.”

Fintan sat silently for a long time. He thought of his exile. Of his family. Of the years he had spent clawing his way back to honor. Of the forest. Of the gods he prayed to who rarely answered. Finally he spoke. “You are not Cernunnos.”

“No.”

“You are not the Mór-Rioghan.”

“Definitely not.”

“Then why me?”

She smiled again. “Because the other gods test people. I bet on them. And I believe you are a very good wager.”

Fintan slowly placed his hand over the cards. “I will protect travelers,” he said. “I will walk dangerous roads. I will not turn away from chance. And when the game begins… I will play.”

The woman clapped happily. “Excellent!” The table vanished. The forest vanished. The cards burned with green light.

Fintan woke in the guild hall. Sweat soaked his clothes. The guild master was sitting nearby. “You’re awake,” the man said. “You’ve been unconscious for three days.”

Fintan slowly sat up. Something rested in his hands. A deck of playing cards, carved from pale wood and etched with impossibly intricate symbols. The guild master frowned. “That wasn’t there before.”

Fintan turned one card between his fingers. For the briefest moment, he felt the faintest whisper of laughter, carried on the wind.

As the guild master turned away, one of the cards slipped from the deck and landed face-up on the table. The image showed a jester standing on a tree branch above a sleeping hunter. Fintan smiled faintly beneath his mask. Somewhere in the unseen world, Lady Luck whispered: “Let’s see how this hand plays out.”

# Afterthoughts 
### Lady Luck’s Private Note on Fintan Saoirse Fionn

> Ah, my dear Fintan… or should I call him Dorcha, or Súileabháin, or simply my favorite accident? Names, after all, are just another gamble. I chose him, yes, but not for the reasons he will ever guess.
> 
> Why him? Well… who else would tumble through life with such exquisite imperfection? Blind, yet seeing farther than most who claim sight. Scarred, yet unbroken. Distrusted, yet inherently loyal. He has already played the games I enjoy — betrayal, exile, misfortune — and he did not quit. Most would have. Most would have screamed at the unfairness of the dice. But he… adapted. And oh, how he adapted.
> 
> He walks roads others cannot perceive, hears truths hidden in whispers, feels currents of chance invisible to the ordinary. He is chaos in measured steps — the perfect instrument for a little… subtle intervention. Yes, I do love the subtle. And through him, I can tip fortunes, unobserved, unseen. Perfectly mischievous, perfectly hidden.
> 
> And yet — the real reason? Because he is delightfully unpredictable. I never know what he will do. He might follow a path, or he might shuffle it into something new, and that, my dear chroniclers of the future, is the essence of luck itself. To serve me, one must be a player first, a gambler, a whisper of chance. Fintan… he is all that, wrapped in a cloak of discipline that fools everyone else into thinking he is reliable. How delicious.
> 
> Ah, and let’s not forget the fun. I delight in watching him learn the rules while never fully understanding them. One day, perhaps, he will stumble upon the hidden clause, the secret wager… but not yet. That is my little jest, my quiet game. He will never see me smiling when he loses or wins — though he will feel it, oh yes, he will feel it. And that is enough.
> 
> So, Fintan, my wandering card, my faithful deck-shuffler… play well. Or poorly. I care not. Either way, the game is mine to watch.”

— Lady Luck Erevan Ilesere, The Green Changeling, The Few Jester
